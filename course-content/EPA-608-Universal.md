# EPA 608 Universal Certification - All Refrigerant Types
## Course Curriculum

**Course Duration:** 24 hours  
**Target Audience:** HVAC technicians working on all system types (residential, commercial, industrial)  
**Certification:** EPA Section 608 Universal Technician Certification  
**Passing Score:** 18-25 points on Core + 18-25 points on each Type section

---

## COURSE OVERVIEW

This comprehensive course prepares technicians to pass the EPA Section 608 Universal certification exam, the most comprehensive EPA certification. Universal certification allows technicians to work on ANY refrigeration or air conditioning system containing CFCs, HCFCs, or HFCs. This course covers the Core exam plus all three Type exams (Small Appliances, High-Pressure, and Low-Pressure systems).

---

## PART 1: CORE EXAM TOPICS (6 hours)

### MODULE 1: ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACTS & REGULATIONS (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Ozone layer destruction and stratospheric ozone depletion
- CFC, HCFC, HFC refrigerant identification and ODP ranking
- Clean Air Act requirements and CFC/R-22 phaseout dates
- Montreal Protocol international agreement
- Venting prohibition and maximum penalties ($40,000 per violation)
- Section 608 regulations and equipment coverage

**Key Concepts:**
- Why CFCs and HCFCs harm the ozone layer
- Health effects: UV exposure, skin cancer, cataracts, immune suppression
- Substitute refrigerants and their limitations
- Sales restrictions on refrigerants and recovery equipment

### MODULE 2: REFRIGERATION FUNDAMENTALS (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Refrigeration cycle: compressor, condenser, expansion device, evaporator
- Pressure-temperature relationships and saturation
- Refrigerant states: liquid, vapor, superheated, subcooled
- Refrigeration gauges: compound, high-pressure, vacuum
- Gauge color coding and safe pressure limits
- Leak detection methods: visual, bubble solution, electronic, dye

**Key Concepts:**
- How cooling occurs through latent heat of vaporization
- Identifying refrigerant type using P-T charts
- Proper gauge selection and reading
- Safe pressure testing with nitrogen (never oxygen)

### MODULE 3: THREE R'S & RECOVERY BASICS (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Recover: removing refrigerant from equipment and storing in cylinder
- Recycle: cleaning and filtering recovered refrigerant for reuse
- Reclaim: processing refrigerant to virgin specifications (AHRI 700-2016)
- Recovery equipment types: system-dependent vs. self-contained
- Third-party certification requirements
- Factors affecting recovery speed: temperature, equipment size, hose diameter
- Dehydration and evacuation requirements
- Avoiding refrigerant mixing

**Key Concepts:**
- Difference between recovery, recycling, and reclamation
- When to use each process
- Proper documentation and record-keeping
- Environmental compliance

## PART 2: TYPE I - SMALL APPLIANCES (4 hours)

### MODULE 4: SMALL APPLIANCE SYSTEMS (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Small appliance definition: hermetically sealed systems
- Examples: refrigerators, freezers, window A/C, dehumidifiers
- System components and refrigerant state at each point
- Typical charge sizes (less than 5 pounds)
- R-12 (CFC) vs. R-134a (HFC) systems
- Equipment age and recovery requirements (pre/post November 15, 1993)

**Recovery Procedures:**
- Working compressor: operate during recovery, install high/low access valves
- Inoperative compressor: heat, strike, use vacuum pump with passive recovery
- Required evacuation: 4 inches mercury vacuum
- Typical recovery time: 15-30 minutes
- Post-recovery: remove access fittings, proper labeling

### MODULE 5: SMALL APPLIANCE SAFETY & OILS (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Decomposition products of refrigerants at high temperatures
- Lubricant compatibility: mineral oil (R-12) vs. ester oil (R-134a)
- Fractionation in blended refrigerants
- Personal protective equipment for small appliance work
- Cylinder safety and storage
- Nitrogen pressure testing procedures

---

## PART 3: TYPE II - HIGH-PRESSURE SYSTEMS (6 hours)

### MODULE 6: HIGH-PRESSURE SYSTEM FUNDAMENTALS (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- High-pressure system definition: pressure >150 psig
- Examples: air conditioning, commercial refrigeration, heat pumps
- System components: compressor, condenser, receiver, expansion device, evaporator
- Refrigerant types: R-22 (HCFC), R-410A (HFC blend), R-454B (low-GWP)
- Pressure-temperature relationships for common refrigerants
- Identifying refrigerant in systems: label, P-T method, color coding

**Key Concepts:**
- Receiver vs. accumulator function
- Superheat and subcooling calculations
- System performance indicators
- Pressure limits for different refrigerants

### MODULE 7: HIGH-PRESSURE LEAK DETECTION & REPAIR (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Leak detection signs: excessive superheat, oil traces, performance loss
- Leak detection methods: nitrogen with trace refrigerant, electronic detectors
- Allowable leak rates: commercial/industrial vs. other appliances
- Leak repair requirements and timeframes
- Major vs. non-major repairs
- Leak repair recordkeeping and documentation
- Extensions for complex repairs

**Repair Requirements by System Size:**
- <50 lbs refrigerant: no specific leak rate limit
- >50 lbs refrigerant: 15% per year allowable leak rate
- Commercial/industrial: 35% per year allowable leak rate

### MODULE 8: HIGH-PRESSURE RECOVERY & EVACUATION (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Recovery techniques: liquid recovery first (speeds process)
- Chilling recovery vessel and heating appliance to speed recovery
- Reducing cross-contamination with new refrigerants
- Checking for remaining liquid after reaching vacuum
- Recovery requirements based on system conditions:
  - Disposal: 0 inches mercury vacuum
  - Major repair, leaky system: 0 inches mercury vacuum
  - Major repair, non-leaky system: 10 inches mercury vacuum
  - Non-major repair: 4 inches mercury vacuum
- Equipment age considerations (pre/post November 15, 1993)
- Prohibition on system-dependent equipment for >15 lb systems

## PART 4: TYPE III - LOW-PRESSURE SYSTEMS (4 hours)

### MODULE 9: LOW-PRESSURE SYSTEM FUNDAMENTALS (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Low-pressure system definition: pressure <150 psig
- Examples: chillers, low-pressure centrifugal compressors
- Typical refrigerants: R-11 (CFC), R-123 (HCFC), R-245fa (HFC)
- System components and operation
- Pressure-temperature relationships
- Identifying refrigerants in low-pressure systems

**Key Concepts:**
- Vacuum operation and atmospheric pressure effects
- Moisture and air contamination risks
- System protection requirements

### MODULE 10: LOW-PRESSURE RECOVERY & SAFETY (2 hours)

**Topics:**
- Recovery procedures for low-pressure systems
- Evacuation requirements: 25 inches mercury vacuum (very deep vacuum)
- Equipment requirements: self-contained recovery equipment
- System-dependent equipment not allowed
- Moisture removal: critical for low-pressure systems
- Dehydration procedures
- Safety considerations for low-pressure work
- Atmospheric pressure effects on recovery speed

## PART 5: SUBSTITUTE REFRIGERANTS & OILS (2 hours)

### MODULE 11: ADVANCED REFRIGERANT & LUBRICANT TOPICS

**Topics:**
- No "drop-in" refrigerant replacements exist
- System modifications required for new refrigerants
- Low-GWP refrigerants: R-410A, R-32, R-454B, R-290, R-600a
- Flammability classifications and safety requirements
- Lubricant compatibility matrix
- Mineral oil, alkylbenzene, ester, PAO oils
- Proper oil selection for each refrigerant
- Oil capacity and charging procedures
- Fractionation in blended refrigerants

**Compatibility Chart:**
- R-12: mineral oil
- R-22: mineral oil or alkylbenzene
- R-134a: ester or polyol ester
- R-410A: ester or polyol ester
- R-32: ester or polyol ester
- Hydrocarbons (R-290, R-600a): ester or polyol ester
