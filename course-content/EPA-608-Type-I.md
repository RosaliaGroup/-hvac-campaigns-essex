# EPA 608 Type I Certification - Small Appliances
## Course Curriculum

**Course Duration:** 12 hours  
**Target Audience:** HVAC technicians working with small appliances (refrigerators, freezers, window A/C units)  
**Certification:** EPA Section 608 Type I Technician Certification  
**Passing Score:** 18-25 points on Core + Type I sections

---

## COURSE OVERVIEW

This course prepares technicians to pass the EPA Section 608 Type I certification exam, required by federal law for anyone who maintains, services, repairs, or disposes of small appliances containing refrigerants. Students will learn environmental regulations, refrigerant handling, recovery techniques, and safety protocols specific to small appliance systems.

---

## MODULE 1: ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACTS & REGULATIONS (2 hours)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand ozone depletion and its environmental consequences
- Identify different refrigerant types and their ozone-depletion potential
- Know the Clean Air Act and Montreal Protocol requirements
- Understand venting prohibitions and penalties

### Topics Covered

**Ozone Layer and Refrigerants**
- How chlorine in CFCs and HCFCs destroys stratospheric ozone
- Ozone-depletion potential (ODP) ranking: CFCs > HCFCs > HFCs
- Identification of refrigerant types: R-12 (CFC), R-22 (HCFC), R-134a (HFC)
- Health effects of ozone depletion (UV radiation exposure, skin cancer, cataracts)

**Clean Air Act Requirements**
- CFC phaseout date (January 1, 1996)
- R-22 phaseout date (January 1, 2020)
- Venting prohibition: Cannot release refrigerant to atmosphere during servicing or disposal
- Maximum penalty: $40,000 per violation
- Sales restrictions on refrigerants and recovery equipment

**Montreal Protocol**
- International agreement to phase out ozone-depleting substances
- Signatory countries and compliance timeline
- Role of substitute refrigerants (HFCs, HFOs)

### Practice Questions
- Which refrigerant has the highest ODP?
- What is the penalty for venting refrigerant?
- What is the CFC phaseout date?

## MODULE 2: REFRIGERATION FUNDAMENTALS (2 hours)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand the refrigeration cycle and pressure-temperature relationships
- Identify refrigerant states (liquid vs. vapor)
- Use refrigeration gauges correctly
- Detect leaks in small appliances

### Topics Covered

**Refrigeration Cycle**
- Four main components: compressor, condenser, expansion device, evaporator
- Refrigerant state at each point in the cycle
- Pressure and temperature relationships
- How cooling occurs (latent heat of vaporization)

**Pressure-Temperature Relationships**
- Saturation pressure and temperature
- Superheat and subcooling concepts
- Using P-T charts for common refrigerants (R-12, R-134a)
- Identifying refrigerant type using pressure and temperature

**Refrigeration Gauges**
- Gauge types: compound gauge, high-pressure gauge, vacuum gauge
- Color coding: red (high pressure), blue (low pressure), yellow (compound)
- Proper gauge range selection
- Safe pressure limits for different refrigerants
- Reading and interpreting gauge values

**Leak Detection Methods**
- Visual inspection for oil traces
- Bubble solution method
- Electronic leak detectors
- Dye-enhanced refrigerant method
- Nitrogen pressure test (with trace refrigerant only, never oxygen)

### Practice Questions
- At what point in the cycle is refrigerant a liquid?
- What does high superheat indicate?
- How do you safely detect leaks in a small appliance?

## MODULE 3: SMALL APPLIANCE RECOVERY (3 hours)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand small appliance definition and characteristics
- Perform proper recovery procedures for appliances with working compressors
- Recover refrigerant from appliances with inoperative compressors
- Identify and use appropriate recovery equipment

### Topics Covered

**Small Appliance Definition**
- Hermetically sealed systems (compressor sealed in shell)
- Typical examples: refrigerators, freezers, window A/C units, dehumidifiers
- Charge size: typically less than 5 pounds
- System-dependent vs. self-contained recovery equipment

**Recovery Equipment Requirements**
- Equipment manufactured before November 15, 1993 vs. after
- Different evacuation requirements based on equipment age
- System-dependent ("passive") recovery devices
- Self-contained recovery units
- Third-party certification requirements (AHRI Standard 700-2016)

**Recovery Procedures - Working Compressor**
- Install high and low-side access valves
- Connect recovery equipment to both high and low sides
- Operate compressor during recovery
- Monitor pressure gauges until system reaches required vacuum
- Typical evacuation requirement: 4 inches of mercury vacuum
- Time required: 15-30 minutes depending on system size

**Recovery Procedures - Inoperative Compressor**
- Heat the compressor (carefully, not exceeding 130°F)
- Sharply strike the compressor to dislodge liquid
- Use vacuum pump with non-pressurized recovery container
- Install both high and low-side access valves
- Manual recovery techniques for passive systems
- Proper handling of non-condensables

**Refrigerant Identification**
- R-12 (CFC) vs. R-134a (HFC) identification
- Pressure-temperature method
- Visual identification of system components
- Preventing cross-contamination

**Post-Recovery Procedures**
- Remove solderless access fittings at conclusion
- Proper labeling of recovered refrigerant
- Documentation and record-keeping
- Disposal of non-recoverable refrigerant

### Practice Questions
- What is the required evacuation level for small appliances?
- How do you recover refrigerant from an appliance with an inoperative compressor?
- What is the difference between system-dependent and self-contained recovery equipment?

## MODULE 4: SUBSTITUTE REFRIGERANTS & OILS (1.5 hours)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand why "drop-in" replacements don't exist
- Know lubricant compatibility requirements
- Identify appropriate oils for different refrigerants
- Prevent fractionation problems in blended refrigerants

### Topics Covered

**Substitute Refrigerants**
- R-134a as replacement for R-12
- Why drop-in replacements don't work
- System modifications required for new refrigerants
- Pressure and temperature differences

**Lubricant Compatibility**
- CFC/HCFC systems use mineral oil
- HFC systems (R-134a) require ester or polyol ester oils
- Incompatibility issues: oil breakdown, acid formation, corrosion
- Proper oil identification and selection
- Oil capacity and charging procedures

**Fractionation Problem**
- Blended refrigerants separate during leakage
- Different components leak at different rates
- System performance degradation
- Importance of complete recovery and recharging

### Practice Questions
- Can you use R-134a directly in an R-12 system?
- What type of oil is used with R-134a?
- What is fractionation and why is it a problem?

## MODULE 5: SAFETY & HAZARDS (2 hours)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand health hazards of refrigerant exposure
- Know proper personal protective equipment
- Understand cylinder safety and storage
- Prevent accidents and injuries

### Topics Covered

**Refrigerant Health Hazards**
- Oxygen deprivation (asphyxiation risk)
- Cardiac sensitization (sudden death syndrome)
- Frostbite from liquid refrigerant contact
- Long-term health effects
- Proper ventilation requirements

**Personal Protective Equipment**
- Gloves (nitrile or leather)
- Safety glasses or goggles
- Respirator or SCBA in extreme cases
- Proper clothing and footwear
- First aid procedures for exposure

**Cylinder Safety**
- Reusable (recovery) cylinders vs. disposable cylinders
- DOT approval requirements
- Yellow and gray color coding (reusable cylinders)
- Never refill disposable cylinders
- Maximum fill level: 80% by weight
- Proper cylinder storage and handling

**Nitrogen Safety**
- Use nitrogen only for pressure testing (never oxygen or compressed air)
- Oxygen explosion hazard
- Proper nitrogen regulator with relief valve
- Pressure limits for nitrogen testing
- Nitrogen disposal procedures

**Shipping and Labeling**
- Refrigerant cylinder labels
- DOT classification tags
- Proper documentation
- Transportation regulations
- Storage requirements

### Practice Questions
- What are the health hazards of refrigerant exposure?
- What is the maximum fill level for a recovery cylinder?
- Why should you never use oxygen for pressure testing?

## FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

**Q: How long is the EPA 608 Type I certification valid?**  
A: Lifetime. There is no renewal requirement for EPA 608 certification.

**Q: Can I work on commercial systems with Type I certification?**  
A: No. Type I is limited to small appliances. Commercial systems require Type II or Universal certification.

**Q: What if I fail the EPA exam?**  
A: You can retake the exam. Most testing organizations allow retakes after a waiting period.

**Q: Do I need formal training to take the EPA exam?**  
A: No, but this course significantly improves your chances of passing on the first attempt.

**Q: Is this course recognized by employers?**  
A: Yes. Employers value EPA 608 certification and this course demonstrates your commitment to professional development.
