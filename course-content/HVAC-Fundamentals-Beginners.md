## MODULE 4: REFRIGERATION CYCLE BASICS (2.5 hours)

### Learning Objectives
- Understand the basic refrigeration cycle
- Identify system components
- Explain how cooling occurs
- Apply cycle concepts to system operation

### Topics Covered

**What is Refrigeration?**
- Definition and principles
- Heat transfer concepts
- Latent heat and sensible heat
- Why refrigeration is needed

**The Four-Part Refrigeration Cycle**

**1. Compressor (Pumping)**
- Compresses low-pressure vapor to high-pressure vapor
- Increases temperature
- Creates pressure difference
- Driven by electric motor

**2. Condenser (Cooling)**
- High-pressure vapor enters
- Cools and condenses to liquid
- Heat is rejected to outdoor air
- Pressure remains high

**3. Expansion Device (Throttling)**
- Reduces pressure suddenly
- Creates temperature drop
- Liquid becomes partially vapor
- Prepares refrigerant for evaporator

**4. Evaporator (Heating)**
- Low-pressure liquid/vapor mixture enters
- Absorbs heat from indoor air
- Completely vaporizes to low-pressure vapor
- Cools the building

**Pressure and Temperature**
- Pressure-temperature relationship
- Saturation point
- Superheat and subcooling basics
- Why these matter

**Refrigerant States**
- Liquid: dense, flows easily
- Vapor: gas, expands to fill space
- Superheated vapor: above saturation temperature
- Subcooled liquid: below saturation temperature

**System Components Overview**
- Compressor: the "heart" of the system
- Condenser: outdoor unit (usually)
- Expansion device: controls flow
- Evaporator: indoor unit (usually)
- Connecting lines: carry refrigerant

### Visual Learning
- Refrigeration cycle diagram
- Component location in real systems
- Pressure-temperature charts
- Animation of cycle operation

### Practice Questions

## MODULE 5: HVAC SYSTEM TYPES (2 hours)

### Learning Objectives
- Identify different HVAC system types
- Understand system components and operation
- Explain heating and cooling methods
- Apply system knowledge to troubleshooting

### Topics Covered

**Residential Systems**

**Window Air Conditioners**
- Single-unit design
- All components in one unit
- Cooling only
- Limited capacity
- Easy installation

**Split-System Air Conditioners**
- Indoor unit (evaporator)
- Outdoor unit (compressor, condenser)
- Connected by refrigerant lines
- Cooling only
- Most common residential system

**Heat Pumps**
- Heating and cooling capability
- Reversing valve changes refrigerant direction
- More efficient than resistance heating
- Backup heat for cold climates

**Furnaces**
- Gas, electric, or oil-fired
- Heats air using combustion or resistance
- Blower distributes heated air
- Usually paired with A/C for cooling

**Commercial Systems**

**Rooftop Units**
- All-in-one package
- Heating and cooling
- Large capacity
- Easy maintenance access

**Chillers**
- Large commercial systems
- Cool water instead of air
- Water circulates through building
- Very efficient for large buildings

**System Components**
- Thermostat: controls temperature
- Blower: moves air
- Ductwork: distributes air
- Filter: removes particles
- Humidifier/dehumidifier: controls moisture

### System Comparison
- Efficiency differences
- Cost differences
- Maintenance requirements
- Lifespan expectations

### Topics Covered

**Refrigerant Types**
- R-12 (CFC): phased out, historical
- R-22 (HCFC): being phased out
- R-134a (HFC): current common
- R-410A (HFC blend): modern systems
- Low-GWP refrigerants: future direction

**Refrigerant Properties**
- Boiling point: determines temperature range
- Pressure: system operating pressure
- Toxicity: health hazards
- Flammability: safety classification
- Compatibility: with oils and components

**Environmental Impact**
- Ozone depletion: CFCs and HCFCs
- Global warming potential (GWP)
- Climate change: HFCs contribute
- Low-GWP alternatives: R-32, R-290, R-600a

**Regulations**
- Clean Air Act: U.S. federal law
- EPA regulations: refrigerant handling
- Montreal Protocol: international agreement
- Venting prohibition: cannot release to atmosphere
- Penalties: up to $40,000 per violation

**Proper Handling**
- Never vent refrigerant
- Recover and recycle
- Proper storage and labeling
- Leak prevention
- Personal safety with refrigerants

## FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

**Q: Do I need prior experience to take this course?**  
A: No. This course is designed for complete beginners with no HVAC experience.

**Q: Will this course get me a job?**  
A: This course provides the foundation. Most employers require EPA 608 certification and hands-on experience. Consider apprenticeship or trade school after this course.

**Q: How long does it take to become a fully certified technician?**  
A: Typically 4-5 years including apprenticeship, EPA 608, NATE, and state licensing.

**Q: What's the difference between EPA 608 and NATE?**  
A: EPA 608 is federal law requirement for refrigerant handling. NATE is industry-recognized certification for overall HVAC competency.

**Q: Can I make good money in HVAC?**  
A: Yes. HVAC technicians earn competitive wages, with experienced technicians earning $70,000-$100,000+/year.

**Q: Is HVAC work physically demanding?**  
A: Yes, it involves climbing, bending, lifting, and working in various conditions. Good physical fitness is helpful.

**Q: Are there opportunities for advancement?**  
A: Yes. You can advance to supervisor, manager, business owner, or specialized roles.
